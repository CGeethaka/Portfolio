PROJECTS / U.S. TRADE & TARIFF IMPACT ANALYSIS
// PROJECT_01.pbix
Power BI DAX Trade Analysis Data Visualisation

U.S. Trade & Tariff Impact Analysis Dashboard

An end-to-end Power BI solution that transforms three complex economic datasets into a single decision-ready dashboard — revealing how U.S. tariff policies in 2024 rippled through global trade balances, commodity prices and bilateral trade relationships.

Project Overview

The U.S.-China trade war and subsequent rounds of tariff escalation from 2018 to 2024 created one of the most complex economic policy environments in modern history. This project was born out of a genuine desire to understand — through data — how those tariffs translated into real-world economic consequences: did trade deficits widen? Did domestic prices rise as economists predicted? Which product categories were most affected?

The answer required pulling together three entirely separate data sources, harmonising them on a common schema, and building a dashboard that allows any analyst or policymaker to slice through the complexity and find the signal in the noise. The result is a fully interactive Power BI report integrating country-level bilateral trade flows, time-series tariff rate schedules, and product-level consumer price index data — all connected by a custom DAX data model built from the ground up.

Problem Statement

Trade policy analysts and business intelligence teams faced a fragmented data landscape. Tariff schedules were stored in one format, bilateral trade statistics in another, and price impact data in a third — with no common key or consistent granularity between them. Existing public dashboards on trade were either too high-level to be actionable, or too technically demanding for non-economists to navigate.

The core challenge this project addressed: How can we translate raw policy data — tariff rates, trade volumes, price indices — into visual intelligence that non-technical stakeholders can act on immediately?

Methodology & Technical Approach

Data Acquisition & Integration: Three datasets were sourced and merged using Power Query. Country-level trade data provided import/export volumes by bilateral pair. Time-series tariff data captured the evolving rate schedules across product categories. Product-level price data tracked consumer-facing price changes. Significant cleaning was required — handling missing values, normalising country codes across schemas, and aligning date granularities from annual to quarterly.

DAX Measure Development: A suite of custom DAX measures was built. Net Trade Balance calculated the difference between exports and imports at both country and product-category level. Deficit per Capita normalised trade deficit figures against population data. Tariff Intensity % expressed the weighted average tariff burden on a bilateral relationship. Price Increase % computed year-over-year change in consumer prices and correlated it with tariff rate changes.

Visualisation Design: The dashboard was designed around three analytical lenses — geographic (map chart showing bilateral trade flows), temporal (area charts showing trade balance and tariff rates over time), and product-level (matrix table showing which categories experienced the greatest price impacts). All visuals are cross-filtered, meaning selecting a country on the map automatically updates the time series and product tables.

Key Findings
Trade Deficit Widened — Not NarrowedDespite the stated policy objective of reducing the U.S. trade deficit, bilateral deficits with several major trading partners actually widened between 2022 and 2024, as importers shifted sourcing to third countries rather than reducing import volumes.
Consumer Price Pass-Through Was SignificantProduct categories with tariff increases above 15% showed an average consumer price increase of 8–12% within two quarters, confirming tariff costs were being partially passed through to end consumers rather than absorbed by manufacturers.
Electronics & Steel Were Most ImpactedConsumer electronics and steel/aluminium products showed the highest Tariff Intensity % scores, and also corresponded with the greatest Price Increase % in the product-level analysis — making them the clearest casualties of the tariff escalation.
Emerging Markets Benefited from Trade DiversionCountries like Vietnam, Mexico and India showed significant increases in export volumes to the U.S. during the same period, suggesting trade diversion effects were substantial — a nuanced finding not visible in high-level headline data.
Challenges & Solutions
01
Harmonising three datasets with different country-coding conventions (ISO2, ISO3, and free-text) required building a custom lookup table in Power Query to resolve all naming conflicts before the data model could be established.
02
DAX time intelligence functions required careful handling of the fiscal calendar vs. calendar year distinction in the tariff schedule data. A custom date table was created to resolve this and ensure accurate year-over-year comparisons.
03
The product-level price data used CPI category codes while the tariff schedule used HTS codes. A manual crosswalk mapping was built to enable joining these two datasets in a meaningful and statistically sound way.
Outcome & Learnings

This project significantly deepened my understanding of building production-grade data models in Power BI — moving beyond simple drag-and-drop visualisations into genuine analytical engineering. The challenge of integrating mismatched datasets is one of the most common real-world problems a Business Analyst faces, and this project gave me extensive hands-on experience with it.

I also developed a strong appreciation for data storytelling — the dashboard was designed so that a non-economist could open it and within 30 seconds understand the core narrative: tariffs changed prices, reshaped trade flows, and created winners and losers across different parts of the global economy.

Housing Prices Inferential Analysis

A rigorous statistical investigation into what drives housing prices — using the classic Boston Housing dataset as the testing ground for building, evaluating and interpreting simple and multiple linear regression models entirely in R.

Project Overview

Housing prices are one of the most economically significant and hotly debated topics in urban economics. Understanding which factors genuinely drive price variation — and which are merely correlated — requires moving beyond intuition into rigorous statistical inference. This project uses the Boston Housing Dataset, a canonical benchmark in the statistics and machine learning community, to explore exactly that question.

The dataset contains 506 observations of Boston-area neighbourhoods, each described by 13 predictor variables including crime rate, proximity to the Charles River, nitric oxide concentration, average number of rooms, distance to employment centres, property tax rate, and the percentage of lower-status population. The project is entirely in R, leveraging base R for statistical modelling and ggplot2 for publication-quality visualisations.

Statistical Methodology

Descriptive Analysis: The analysis began with a thorough exploratory phase — computing summary statistics, assessing distributional shapes through histograms, and identifying potential outliers through boxplots. The target variable (MEDV — median house value) showed a right-skewed distribution with a notable concentration at the $50k ceiling, suggesting data censoring at the upper end.

Correlation Analysis: A full correlation matrix was computed across all 13 predictor variables. Several strong linear relationships emerged: the percentage of lower-status population (LSTAT) showed a strong negative correlation with house prices (r = −0.74), while the average number of rooms (RM) showed a strong positive correlation (r = +0.70). These became natural candidates for the simple linear regression models.

Multiple Linear Regression: A full multiple regression model was built using all 13 predictors. Forward selection and backward elimination were both explored to identify the most parsimonious model. The final selected model included 9 predictors and achieved an Adjusted R² of 0.731 — explaining 73.1% of the variance in median house prices.

Regression Diagnostics: Model assumptions were rigorously evaluated using diagnostic plots: a Residuals vs Fitted plot for linearity and homoscedasticity, a Q-Q plot for normality, a Scale-Location plot for heteroscedasticity, and a Cook's Distance plot to identify influential observations.

Key Findings
LSTAT is the Strongest Single PredictorThe percentage of lower-status population alone accounted for 54% of price variance in simple linear regression (R² = 0.544). Each 1% increase in LSTAT was associated with a $950 decrease in median house value, holding all else equal.
Room Count Has a Non-Linear RelationshipWhile RM showed strong positive correlation, the scatter plot revealed a non-linear pattern at both extremes — very small and very large homes deviated from the linear trend, suggesting a log transformation of RM would improve model fit in a follow-up study.
Riverside Properties Command a Price PremiumProperties bordering the Charles River (CHAS = 1) commanded a statistically significant price premium of approximately $2,690 over comparable non-riverside properties (p = 0.012), even after controlling for all other variables.
Tax Rate & Employment Distance Create Trade-offsHigher property tax rates (TAX) were negatively associated with prices, but this effect was partially offset by proximity to employment centres — well-connected, taxed areas still commanded price premiums over poorly-connected, low-tax areas.
Multicollinearity Required Variable RemovalTAX was dropped from the final model due to a VIF of 9.2, indicating strong multicollinearity with RAD (highway accessibility). Removing it improved model stability without significantly changing predictive accuracy.
Challenges & Solutions
01
The target variable's censoring at $50k (506 observations capped at 50 in the dataset) violated the normality assumption. Sensitivity analysis was conducted both with and without these censored observations to quantify their impact on coefficient estimates.
02
Multicollinearity between TAX, RAD and LSTAT was detected using Variance Inflation Factors. TAX was removed from the final model, and the remaining predictors showed acceptable VIF values below 5.
03
Three high-leverage observations identified via Cook's Distance were found to have disproportionate influence. The model was re-fitted without them — coefficient changes were within 5%, confirming the overall model's robustness to these outliers.
Outcome & Learnings

This project gave me a deep, hands-on understanding of the full inferential statistics pipeline — from exploratory visualisation through to model diagnostics and interpretation. It moved me well beyond "fitting a line" into genuinely understanding what coefficients mean, why assumptions matter, and how to be honest about a model's limitations.

The project also taught me the importance of communicating statistical findings to non-statisticians. The final output wasn't just a model — it was a narrative: here are the factors that matter, here's how much they matter, here's what we can and can't conclude from this data.

// PROJECT_03.ipynb
Python EDA Data Visualisation Pandas Seaborn

Airline Passenger Satisfaction Analysis

A comprehensive exploratory data analysis of 12,981 airline passenger records — uncovering which service dimensions, demographics and flight characteristics are the strongest predictors of customer satisfaction in commercial aviation.

Project Overview

In an industry where customers have abundant choice and switching costs are low, understanding the drivers of passenger satisfaction is directly tied to loyalty, revenue and reputation. Airlines invest heavily in customer experience programmes, but without rigorous data analysis, those investments may be targeted at the wrong levers.

This project analysed a dataset of 12,981 airline passengers, each rated across multiple service dimensions (seat comfort, food quality, online boarding, in-flight entertainment, leg room, baggage handling, check-in service, cleanliness and more), along with demographic variables (age, gender, customer type) and flight characteristics (flight distance, departure delay, arrival delay, travel class).

The analytical goal was to move from raw data to actionable insights — answering questions an airline's customer experience team would genuinely care about: Who is most likely to be dissatisfied? Which service dimensions have the highest impact? Does flight distance change the satisfaction equation?

Analytical Approach

Data Cleaning: The dataset was imported into a Pandas DataFrame and subjected to thorough quality assessment. Missing values were found primarily in the Arrival Delay column (flights with no recorded delay, coded as missing rather than zero) and were imputed with zero after verification. Duplicate records were checked and removed. Categorical variables were label-encoded for downstream correlation analysis.

Univariate Analysis: Each variable was examined individually to understand its distribution. Satisfaction was binary (satisfied vs. neutral/dissatisfied) with a roughly 57/43 split. Service rating variables were ordinal (0–5) and their distributions were plotted as histograms to identify rating clusters and potential ceiling/floor effects.

Bivariate & Multivariate Analysis: The core analysis involved systematically examining the relationship between each potential driver and the satisfaction outcome. Grouped bar charts, KDE plots and violin plots were used to visualise how satisfaction rates varied across demographic segments, travel classes and service rating levels. A correlation heatmap was constructed across all numeric variables to identify multicollinearity and co-varying factors.

Visualisation Strategy: All visualisations were built in Seaborn and Matplotlib using a progressive disclosure principle — starting with the big picture (overall satisfaction rates) and drilling into progressively finer-grained breakdowns. Each chart was annotated with percentage values to make insights immediately readable without requiring axis interpretation.

Key Findings
Business Class Is Dramatically More SatisfiedBusiness class passengers showed a satisfaction rate of 69.4%, compared to 24.1% in Economy and 58.2% in Economy Plus. The gap was most pronounced in in-flight entertainment, seat comfort and food/drink ratings — the dimensions where class experience diverges most sharply.
Online Boarding & Wi-Fi Are the Highest-Impact LeversAmong all service dimensions, online boarding and in-flight Wi-Fi showed the strongest correlation with overall satisfaction. Passengers who rated these 4 or above were 3.2× more likely to report being satisfied overall — despite these being relatively low-cost improvements for airlines.
Departure Delays Have a Clear Threshold EffectSatisfaction dropped sharply for delays exceeding 45 minutes, but minimal differences were observed between 0–30 minute delays and on-time flights. Passengers broadly tolerate minor delays but have a clear psychological threshold at around 45 minutes.
Loyal Customers Are More Tolerant but Not ImmuneReturning/loyal customers showed higher baseline satisfaction (62%) compared to first-time travellers (43%), but were equally affected by departure delays and poor in-flight Wi-Fi — loyalty does not provide full insulation from service failures.
Age Interacts with Flight DistanceYounger passengers (under 35) showed higher satisfaction on short-haul flights but significantly lower satisfaction on long-haul flights (5+ hours), particularly on seat comfort and entertainment. Older passengers showed more consistent satisfaction across distances, reflecting different comfort expectations.
Challenges & Solutions
01
The dataset had a class imbalance between Business and Economy passengers (~40/60 split). Analyses involving class-level comparisons were performed with proportional weighting to avoid the majority class dominating aggregate statistics.
02
Several service dimension variables were highly intercorrelated (e.g., cleanliness and food ratings), making it difficult to attribute satisfaction effects to individual dimensions in isolation. Factor analysis was scoped as a meaningful next step.
03
Arrival and departure delay variables had extreme right-skewed distributions with outliers up to 1,592 minutes. Log-transformation was applied when computing correlation coefficients to reduce the influence of these extreme values on the magnitudes.
Outcome & Learnings

This project was my deepest dive into Python-based data analysis and gave me strong confidence with the full Pandas/Seaborn/Matplotlib stack. Working with a real-world dataset of nearly 13,000 records reinforced the importance of methodical data validation before analysis — assumptions that seem safe often aren't.

The most valuable skill I developed was translating visual patterns into business recommendations. It's one thing to notice that Business class passengers are more satisfied — it's another to articulate that the gap is driven by three specific, actionable service dimensions, and that closing that gap in Economy for these dimensions offers the highest ROI for passenger experience investment.

This project also sparked my interest in building a follow-up classification model — using the satisfaction label as the target and the service dimensions as features — the natural next step from EDA into predictive modelling territory.
